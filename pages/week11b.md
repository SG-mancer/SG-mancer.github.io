# Week 11 AI prompts and responses

## 1

### Prompt
> You are a teacher of Year 11 Timber and Furniture technologies at a rural highschool where typically students have a low rate of success completing major projects in the HSC, the identified approach to improve this is to scaffold students with a focused learning sequence that focuses on teaching the design process to encourage creativity, and improve the quality of working drawings, work-breakdowns (construction steps, cutting lists) and time planning. Most students haven't used CAD before, so the first CAD lesson in this sequence demonstrates in OnShpae creating a New Part Studio, Sketching the piece of stock – draw width (51), length of piece (475) then Extrude thickness (19), using the Transform tool – select Rotate, Edge of Extrude, Angle (12) to rotate the piece as if we were cutting in the mortice, using the Sketch tool and then extrude to remove the mortice hole... and then applying a similiar approach for the tennon joints on the table appron etc. This was going to be done through a step-by-step live demonstration students will follow along with on the school laptops in the workshop, but because this tool (a web-based CAD software OnShape) is new and because many students in the class are prone to off-task behaviour and require support to stay engaged (due to a range of issues including ADHD and dislexia diagnoses of a majority of the students - who were typically more engaged with the Maker approrach of the Stage 5 timber technology subject that was almost all hands on production) can you suggest a brief learning sequence for this 80 minute lesson that moves students from logging into OnShape for possibly the first time, to creating a model of the first part (and maybe the second part) for their side table project.

### Response
This is a great challenge—bridging the hands-on, “maker” energy of your Year 11s with the precision demands of HSC major projects. Given the rural context, low past success rates, ADHD/dyslexia prevalence, and first-time CAD use, your instinct to scaffold heavily is spot on.

Here is a **brief, highly structured 80-minute learning sequence** designed to minimise off-task behaviour, support cognitive load, and leverage the live demonstration approach you planned.

---

## Lesson Title: First CAD Model – Side Table Leg & Mortice

**Lesson Goal:** Students log into OnShape, create one leg with a mortice, and (if time) start the apron with a tenon.

### Materials & Setup (before lesson starts)
- **OnShape accounts created** (teacher does this beforehand using a class sign-up link or Google Classroom).
- **Projection** at front for your live demo.
- **Student laptops** open to the OnShape login page **before the bell** (saves 5-7 critical minutes).
- **A3 printed “Cheat Sheet”** at each bench (visual steps: log in → new Part Studio → sketch rectangle 51x475 → extrude 19 → rotate edge 12° → sketch mortice → extrude remove). Dyslexia-friendly font + icons.
- **Timer displayed** on screen at all times.

---

## Lesson Sequence (80 minutes)

| Time | Activity | Teacher Does | Students Do | Support Strategies |
|------|----------|--------------|-------------|--------------------|
| **0-8 min** | **Entry & Login Sprint** | Greet, direct: “Laptops up. OnShape login page. Go.” Walk around, help with passwords. | Log in. Keep hands on keyboard (no waiting). | Printed logins for dyslexic students. Reward first 3 logged in with a sticker (silly but works). |
| **8-12 min** | **Demo: New Part Studio + Sketch stock** | “Watch first, then copy.” Show: New Part Studio → Sketch rectangle (51,475) → Extrude (19). No talking during demo. | Watch. No mouse movement. | Fidget tools allowed. Demo uses high-contrast cursor zoom. |
| **12-22 min** | **Guided Copy – Step 1 (Leg body)** | “Your turn. 10 minutes.” Project steps on screen. Walk bench to bench. | Create the 51x475x19 block. | Pair weaker students with a ‘bench buddy’. Use 1:1 prompts: “What’s the width again? Look at sheet.” |
| **22-28 min** | **Demo: Rotate edge + Mortice removal** | Show Transform → Rotate edge (12°). Then Sketch on face → draw mortice → Extrude Remove. Verbalise each click: “Now I pick this edge... click Rotate... angle 12... done.” | Watch. | Repeat the rotate step twice slowly. Dyslexic students get a numbered checklist on paper. |
| **28-45 min** | **Guided Copy – Step 2 (Mortice)** | “17 minutes. Make the mortice.” Stay standing. Intervene early with off-task browsing. | Rotate edge, sketch mortice, extrude remove. | **Timer on screen:** 5 min for rotate, 12 min for mortice. Off-task? “Show me your rotate first.” |
| **45-55 min** | **Demo: Apron + Tenon (if time)** | “Only if you finish leg + mortice.” Show new Part Studio for apron. Sketch (width 25, length 400), extrude (19). Then sketch tenon on end → extrude. | Watch or work ahead. | Early finishers try tenon. Others stay on mortice. |
| **55-70 min** | **Independent Work + Checkpoints** | Circulate. Use “3 before me” (ask bench buddy, cheat sheet, then teacher). | Finish leg/mortice. Start apron/tenon. | **Exit ticket requirement:** Show mortice on screen to teacher for verbal check. |
| **70-80 min** | **Wrap-up & Save** | “Stop. Save as ‘Leg_YourName’. Close lids.” Quick recap: “What two tools did we use?” (Extrude, Transform). | Save, close. Answer recap. | Students with high anxiety get a pre-written “I saved” sticker to place on laptop. |

---

## Key Modifications for Your Cohort

### 1. **Reduce cognitive load**
- Only **two CAD tools** this lesson: Extrude (add/remove) and Transform/Rotate.
- No dimensions on the fly – all numbers on cheat sheet (51, 475, 19, 12°).

### 2. **Prevent off-task rabbit holes**
- **No internet browsing** – OnShape in full-screen mode (tell them: “If I see another tab, you close laptop for 2 minutes” – use sparingly but consistently).
- **Physical tokens** – each completed step (sketch done, extrude done) earns a coloured peg on their monitor. Visual progress for ADHD brains.

### 3. **Support dyslexia & working memory**
- Cheat sheet uses **bold, sans-serif font** (Arial), cream paper (reduces glare).
- **Verbal countdowns** before transitions: “Saving in 2 minutes… 1 minute…”
- **Allow audio descriptions** – they can talk themselves through steps quietly.

### 4. **Leverage maker instinct**
- Frame CAD as “**digital timber** – same as cutting a mortice, but we’re telling the machine where to cut.”
- At 75 min, show a **3D print or laser-cut example** of their exact part to make the abstract tangible.

---

## What if they don’t finish the mortice by 70 min?

That’s fine. The **non-negotiable exit ticket** is:  
✅ Leg block extruded (51x475x19)  
✅ At least attempted the rotate tool  

The mortice can be finished next lesson. Better a small win than rushing and breaking their confidence.

---

## First thing next lesson
- Quick 5-min warm up: “Open your leg. Add the mortice if missing. Show me the rotate edge again.”
- Then move to tenon on apron – which is just the **inverse** of what they just did.

You’ve got this. The fact you’re pre-thinking the first CAD lesson this carefully already puts them ahead of past cohorts.

## 2

### Prompt
> In the previous lesson to cover UDL, can you identify and list potential barriers of Engagement, Representation and Action and Expression, and then propose and explain in detail one modification to the lesson to address what you consider the highest priority barrier to tackle. Does noting the diversity of the class's learning needs and the rural setting of the school, change which barrier is most pressing to be addressed.

### Response
This is an excellent reflective question. Applying UDL (Universal Design for Learning) to your rural, high-ADHD/dyslexia, maker-oriented timber class is critical because **the barriers are not in the students—they are in the lesson design**.

Below I first identify specific barriers under the three UDL principles, then nominate the **highest priority barrier** considering the rural context and student diversity, and finally propose **one detailed modification** to the previous lesson to address it.

---

## UDL Barrier Analysis for the OnShape Lesson

### 1. Engagement (The *Why* of Learning)
*Affective networks – motivation, relevance, sustained effort*

| Barrier | Why it happens in this lesson |
|--------|-------------------------------|
| **Low perceived relevance** | Students love hands-on making. CAD feels like “school work” not “real timber work.” Rural students often value practical, immediate outcomes over digital simulation. |
| **Sustained attention difficult** | 80 minutes on screen, step-following, fine motor control with mouse – exhausting for ADHD brains. No physical movement or tactile feedback. |
| **Threat of failure** | First-time CAD users fear “breaking it” or looking slow. Dyslexia amplifies anxiety around reading dimension numbers quickly. |
| **No autonomy** | Strict step-by-step demo leaves no room for their maker instinct to explore or tweak. |

### 2. Representation (The *What* of Learning)
*Recognition networks – perceiving and understanding information*

| Barrier | Why it happens in this lesson |
|--------|-------------------------------|
| **Visual overload** | OnShape interface has icons, menus, part tree, view cube, dimension boxes – overwhelming for dyslexic/ADHD visual processing. |
| **Sequential verbal instructions alone** | Teacher says “click Transform, select Rotate, pick edge…” – students with working memory issues lose the chain after 2-3 steps. |
| **Symbolic/numeric load** | Dimensions (51, 475, 19, 12°) require transposition from screen to keyboard – dyslexic students often reverse digits (15° instead of 51mm). |
| **Abstract spatial reasoning** | Rotating an edge by 12° to simulate a mortice cut requires 3D mental rotation – a known difficulty for some neurodivergent learners. |

### 3. Action & Expression (The *How* of Learning)
*Strategic networks – physical action, planning, monitoring progress*

| Barrier | Why it happens in this lesson |
|--------|-------------------------------|
| **Fine motor + timing load** | Simultaneously tracking demo, moving mouse, clicking small icons, typing numbers – cognitive bottleneck. |
| **No physical analogue** | In workshop, they hold a chisel or mark timber. OnScreen, the connection between “extrude remove” and “cutting a mortice” is invisible. |
| **Error recovery unknown** | If they click wrong icon, they don’t know Undo (Ctrl+Z) or how to backtrack – leading to freeze or task abandonment. |
| **Self-monitoring absent** | No physical check (like “does the chisel fit?”). No built-in moment to compare their model to teacher’s. |

---

## Highest Priority Barrier – Considering Rural Context & Student Diversity

After analysis, the **most pressing barrier** is:

> **Engagement – Low perceived relevance + threat of failure**, specifically the gap between their *maker identity* (“I build things with my hands”) and the *digital task* (“follow steps on a screen”).

### Why this is #1 in your rural, high-needs class:

1. **Rural context** – These students likely chose Timber because they succeed with tangible, practical, place-based work. Abstract CAD threatens their sense of competence. If they disengage in Lesson 1, they may never recover for HSC.

2. **ADHD** – Needs immediate, obvious payoff. CAD’s payoff (accurate working drawings for HSC) is months away. Without a *tactile hook*, attention collapses.

3. **Dyslexia** – Following written/sequential digital instructions increases cognitive load and anxiety. When anxiety rises, engagement plummets.

4. **Low past success rate** – Students enter with an unspoken belief: “I’ll probably fail this like last year’s cohort.” That belief becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy unless the first lesson *overfly* connects to their existing skills.

Without addressing engagement first, Representation and Action supports won’t matter – they’ll mentally check out.

---

## One Detailed Modification to Address This Barrier

### Modification: **“CAD to Hand” Tangible Bridge – Physical Mortice Block as Reference Object**

**What changes:**  
Before any student touches OnShape, each student receives a *physical sample block* of timber (pine offcut, exactly 51mm x 475mm x 19mm) with a **pre-cut mortice and a marked 12° angled face**. The block is rough-sawn, clearly timber. The mortice is chiselled, not perfect – intentionally handmade.

**How the lesson opening changes (first 10 minutes):**

| Original lesson start | Modified start with tangible bridge |
|----------------------|-------------------------------------|
| “Log into OnShape.” | **Hold up the physical block.** “This is a leg. Same as you’d cut in workshop. Feel it. Pass it around.” |
| “We’ll make a rectangle 51x475.” | “See this face? That’s 51mm wide. Run your finger along it. That width. Now look at the end – 19mm thick. You’ve cut these dimensions before.” |
| “Then extrude.” | “The angled top? That’s the 12° we cut before mortising. Watch me – [put block on bench, tilt it]. In CAD, we’ll *rotate the whole piece* just like I tilted this block.” |
| “Then sketch a mortice.” | “Now look inside the mortice. Your finger fits. In CAD, we’ll *remove* material – same as your chisel does. Except the computer doesn’t sweat.” |

**Then, during the demo and guided practice:**

- **Each step in OnShape is paired with pointing to the physical block.**  
  *“Extrude added this timber. See? Solid block.”*  
  *“Rotate edge – same as tipping the block.”*  
  *“Extrude remove – same as chopping out this mortice hole.”*

- **Students keep the physical block on their bench** while working on laptop. They can touch it, turn it over, compare.

- **Exit ticket changes:** “Show me your OnShape mortice – now show me the physical mortice. Do they match orientation?”

---

### Why This Modification Works (UDL evidence)

| UDL Principle | How the modification helps |
|---------------|----------------------------|
| **Engagement** | Converts abstract CAD into *extended workshop tool*. Affirms maker identity. Reduces threat (“I already understand this block – CAD is just describing it”). |
| **Representation** | Provides *tactile + visual + verbal* input. Students with dyslexia can feel the 12° angle rather than just read it. Physical block acts as an external working memory aid. |
| **Action & Expression** | Students can physically manipulate the block while planning their next click. Reduces fine motor load because cognitive load is shared between hand and screen. |

---

### Rural Context Specificity

In a metropolitan school, you might assume students have prior digital modeling exposure. In your rural setting:

- **Timber is the primary competency** – leverage it shamelessly. The physical block says: *“You already know this. CAD is just translating your skill.”*
- **Limited tech support** – if OnShape crashes, the block still teaches the geometry. The lesson doesn’t die with wifi dropouts.
- **Workshop culture** – These students trust timber, not software. The block is a *trust bridge*.

---

### Practical Notes for Implementation

- **Make 30 blocks once** (table saw + chisel mortice + mark 12° bevel) – keep in a box for every future CAD lesson.
- **Write “Mortice side” and “12° face”** in sharpie on the block for dyslexic visual reference.
- **If a student is completely lost on screen** – “Put mouse down. Turn the block in your hands. Tell me what shape the mortice is. Now find that face in OnShape.”

This single modification – **physical reference object** – transforms CAD from a threatening abstraction into a digital mirror of their existing maker competence. That’s the engagement hook your cohort needs.

## 3

### Prompt
> A fellow teacher identifies that your solution to improve engagement misses the point that this is meant to help students move ideas from conceptual, not yet existing objects - for example what will in the future be their major works, to being. The tangible already finished products creates is likely to create push-back or students to simply ask, why don't we just get the drawings you already used to make this product, and is very likely to derail the learning intention of the whole process of drawing our own working drawings that then are used in planning and then construction of this scaffolded project. With that intention in mind, do you think this is a risk worth taking pedagogically to support students, or would you instead focus on another engagement barrier for example the Threat of Failure, or Autonomy to support student engagement in this project?

### Response
This is a sharp and pedagogically valuable critique. Your colleague is correct that handing students a *finished* physical mortice block risks short-circuiting the very design process you are trying to teach. If the object already exists, why draw it? That question is lethal to your learning intention.

Let me re-evaluate honestly.

---

## Was the Physical Block Modification a Mistake?

**Yes, as originally described.** It creates:

1. **A "reverse engineering" trap** – Students think: "The teacher already solved this. I'm just copying a pre-existing thing." That's not designing; that's tracing.
2. **Undermines the HSC outcome** – Major works require students to conceive *new* objects from *nothing but a design brief*. Giving a finished block models the wrong cognitive path.
3. **Pushback is predictable** – Rural, maker-oriented students will absolutely say: "Why don't you just give us the drawings?" That derails the whole scaffold.

Your colleague is right to flag this.

---

## Revised Priority: Threat of Failure Over Tangible Bridge

Given the learning intention (move from *conceptual not-yet-existing* to *working drawings*), the **highest priority engagement barrier** shifts from "low relevance" to:

> **Threat of failure** – specifically, the terror of the blank canvas.

For students with ADHD, dyslexia, and a history of low success:

- A blank Part Studio in OnShape = a blank sheet of paper = a history of past failures.
- The question is not "why do this?" but *"what if I can't? What if my mortice is wrong? What if I waste 80 minutes and have nothing?"*

That fear drives avoidance, off-task behaviour, and the "just give us the drawings" demand.

---

## Alternative Modification: Addressing Threat of Failure + Autonomy

Instead of a finished block, use a **low-stakes, partial, deliberately imperfect starter** that preserves the design process.

### Modification: **The "Broken CAD File" Warm-Up**

**What changes:**  
Before students create anything new, they open a **pre-made, deliberately flawed OnShape Part Studio** that you have shared to their account. The file contains:

- A leg block (correct dimensions: 51 x 475 x 19)
- A mortice sketch *but not extruded*
- An apron block *without the tenon*
- One intentional error: the 12° rotate is missing from the leg

**First 15 minutes of lesson:**

| Time | Activity | Why it reduces threat of failure |
|------|----------|--------------------------------|
| 0-5 min | "Open the file called 'Side Table – START HERE'. It's already got a leg. But it's wrong – the top isn't angled. Your job: fix it." | **No blank screen.** Something exists already. Failure is impossible because they are *repairing*, not originating. |
| 5-12 min | "Add the rotate yourself. Use the Transform tool – same as we demo." | **One clear target.** ADHD brain doesn't scan infinite possibilities – just one missing step. |
| 12-15 min | "Now the mortice sketch is there – but it's not a hole yet. Extrude Remove to cut it out." | **Dyslexia support:** dimensions already entered correctly in sketch. They only need to pick "remove" and click. |

**Then transition to autonomy (15-40 min):**  
"Now close that file. Open a *new* Part Studio. Make your OWN leg, with the rotate, with the mortice – but here's the twist: your mortice position can be *different* from mine. Where would YOU put it for strength?"

---

### Why This Works Better

| Barrier addressed | How |
|------------------|-----|
| **Threat of failure** | The broken file makes success virtually certain. First wins build confidence. |
| **Blank canvas anxiety** | Students never face "nothing." They face "something incomplete" – a puzzle, not a void. |
| **Autonomy** | After repairing, they create *their own* with one degree of freedom (mortice position). Low-risk choice. |
| **Preserves design process** | They are still conceiving, still making decisions. The teacher hasn't pre-solved the whole object. |
| **Answers "why not give us drawings"** | "Because the drawings I'd give you have mistakes. You're better than my mistakes." |

---

## Is the Risk of the Finished Block Worth Taking?

**No.** Your colleague is right. The finished block undermines the pedagogical spine of your HSC scaffold: *moving from concept to working drawings to construction.*

However, the *intuition* behind the block was correct – students need a tactile reference. The fix is to make the reference **incomplete, generic, or non-spatial** rather than finished.

### Alternative Tactile Reference That Preserves Design Process

Instead of a finished mortice block, provide:

1. **A bare 51x475x19 offcut – no mortice, no angle**  
   - "This is your starting timber. Your job is to figure out where the mortice goes, what angle the top needs, and draw THAT in CAD."
   - The block models *unworked timber* – the conceptual starting point.

2. **A plastic template with a movable mortice window**  
   - A clear acrylic sheet with a 10mm x 40mm cutout that slides. Students position it on the block to decide mortice location.
   - Low-tech, tactile, but requires *their decision*.

3. **Your own hand-drawn sketch (not CAD)**  
   - Show a rough, messy pencil sketch of a leg with a mortice drawn in pen over an erased area.
   - "See the eraser marks? I changed my mind three times. That's design. Your CAD will be cleaner than my sketch."

---

## Final Verdict

| Approach | Risk to learning intention | Value for engagement | Recommendation |
|----------|---------------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Finished physical mortice block | **High** – implies design is copying | Medium | **Do not use** |
| Broken CAD file (repair then create) | **None** – still requires original decision-making | High | **Use** |
| Bare timber offcut (unworked) | **None** – models conceptual starting point | Medium | **Use as supplement** |
| Threat-of-failure focus via repair-first | **None** – builds confidence without pre-solving | High | **Primary modification** |

Your colleague did you a favour. The broken CAD file gives you the engagement win (no blank screen, no failure) without sacrificing the design process. That's the better path.
