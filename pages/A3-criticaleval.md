# Critical Evaluation


_"Why should we incorporate the use of digital technologies in our classrooms, and what are the key considerations you must consider as an education professional?"_

The imperative to incorporate digital technologies into classrooms is often accepted uncritically, yet my experience in ESC407 revealed that how we integrate technology matters more than whether we integrate it. A constructivist approach, where educators act as facilitators of self-discovery and social negotiation, offers genuine pedagogical value (Bower, 2017). Digital tools can support collaborative meaning-making, but only when deployed with clear educational intent. However, my course's insistence on using multiple proprietary platforms – comic strip generators, AI image generators, and virtual whiteboards – lacked deeper critical consideration of their demonstrable benefits over open or more ethical alternatives. This echoes a wider problem: the platformisation of education, driven by datafication and predictive surveillance, redefines teaching as data curation rather than critical dialogue (Perrotta & Pangrazio, 2023). When technology use becomes performative rather than purposeful, we miss the opportunity to model the very critical evaluation students need. International evidence reinforces this caution: Sweden's recent pivot away from classroom digitisation followed concerns about screen time, distraction, and declining foundational skills, with researchers suggesting digital aids should only be introduced at an age when they encourage, rather than hinder, learning (Cohen, 2026).

One underexplored opportunity is inviting students themselves into critical evaluation of digital tools. Rather than merely consuming platforms, learners could audit the technologies they use: asking who designed this, what data does it collect, and what assumptions does it make about me? Falloon (2020) suggests digital literacy includes personal and societal considerations, yet students rarely get to interrogate the tools shaping their learning. A classroom activity comparing proprietary and open source software, or testing whether an AI image generator can represent their own cultural context, would model exactly the kind of critical digital literacy students need.

Key considerations for education professionals begin with ethical scrutiny, balanced against a clear-eyed assessment of technologies’ capabilities. Generative AI offers potential as an assistive tool, such as simplifying text or offering multiple explanations. However, Rappa et al. (2026) found students with disabilities experienced significant problems: ChatGPT drifted off topic, gave confusing responses, and lacked adaptive support, becoming both an enabler and a constraint (pp. 9-11). For precise, hands-on learning in my Technology classrooms, I would not rely on GenAI for core instruction. Its next-token prediction lacks causal reasoning about physical processes. Instead, specialised tools like Building Information Modelling or Robotic Process Automation offer more reliable, context-aware support (Grunwald et al., 2024). Buchanan (2019) reminds us that ethical dilemmas around student data ownership and commercialisation persist. A thoughtful professional therefore asks not simply "can this tool do something?" but rather: what specific pedagogical problem am I solving, and does this tool's benefit genuinely outweigh its limitations?

Another essential consideration is the situated nature of digital literacy. Falloon (2020) argues students need a holistic view encompassing personal and societal considerations beyond technical procedures. In my Design and Technology classroom, this means teaching students to recognise how technologies embody historical and cultural biases—such as Western conventions in CNC machining or legacy limitations like FORTRAN's 80-character fields. Without this critical awareness, digital literacy reduces to procedural proficiency, missing the opportunity for students to understand technology as a human artefact shaped by values, context, and history.

A professional approach prioritises pedagogical outcomes over tool novelty. Undo buttons and polished automated outputs can erode student resilience, replacing the constructivist work of adapting to mistakes with easy erasure (Bower, 2017). This aligns with the Alice Springs Declaration goal of supporting young people to become active, informed community members (Department of Education, 2019). Whether using AI image generators or marking tools, the professional question is never simply "can I use this?" but rather: does this tool build the adaptive, reflective capacities students need beyond the classroom?
